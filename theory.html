Theoretical Background

Couplings and Kantorovich Optimal Transport

Given two discrete probability distributions \(a \in \mathbb{R}^n\) and \(b \in \mathbb{R}^m\) (non-negative vectors summing to one), a coupling \(P\) is a non-negative \(n \times m\) matrix whose row and column marginals match \(a\) and \(b\): \[U(a,b) = \bigl\{P\ge 0 : P\mathbf{1}_m = a,\; P^{\!\top}\mathbf{1}_n = b\bigr\}.\]

A cost matrix \(C \in \mathbb{R}^{n\times m}\) encodes the cost of moving one unit of mass from position \(x_i\) to position \(y_j\); a typical choice is \(C_{ij} = |x_i - y_j|^p\). The Kantorovich problem finds the coupling of least total cost: \[\min_{P\in U(a,b)} \langle C, P \rangle = \sum_{i,j} C_{ij}\,P_{ij}.\] This is a linear programme and can be solved exactly, but its cost scales super-cubically with the support size, motivating faster approximations.

Entropic Regularisation

Adding a Shannon entropy term \(H(P) = -\sum_{i,j} P_{ij}(\log P_{ij} - 1)\) yields the entropically regularised problem: \[\min_{P\in U(a,b)} \langle C, P \rangle - \varepsilon\, H(P).\] Equivalently, this minimises the KL divergence \(\varepsilon\,\mathrm{KL}(P\,\|\,e^{-C/\varepsilon})\) subject to the marginal constraints. Entropic regularisation brings several advantages:

  • Unique solution. The KL divergence is strictly convex, so the regularised problem has exactly one minimiser.
  • Smoother coupling. More entries of \(P\) are non-zero, producing a diffuse plan rather than a sparse one.
  • Interpolation. As \(\varepsilon \to 0\) the solution converges to an unregularised OT plan; as \(\varepsilon \to \infty\) it tends to the independent product \(a \otimes b\).

Sinkhorn Algorithm

The first-order optimality conditions of the regularised problem show that any solution takes the form \[P^* = \operatorname{diag}(u)\;K\;\operatorname{diag}(v),\] where \(K = e^{-C/\varepsilon}\) is the Gibbs kernel and \(u \in \mathbb{R}^n\), \(v \in \mathbb{R}^m\) are positive scaling vectors. The Sinkhorn algorithm finds these vectors by alternating: \[u \leftarrow \frac{a}{Kv}, \qquad v \leftarrow \frac{b}{K^{\!\top}u},\] where division is elementwise. Because \(K\) is strictly positive, these updates converge linearly to the unique solution.

In practice the iteration stops when the change in \(v\) drops below a tolerance (here \(10^{-7}\)), or after a maximum number of iterations. The transport cost \(\langle C, P\rangle\) is then evaluated directly from the recovered plan.

Role of the Regularisation Parameter \(\varepsilon\)

The parameter \(\varepsilon > 0\) controls the trade-off between cost fidelity and smoothness:

  • Large \(\varepsilon\): the coupling spreads mass broadly, approaching the independent product \(a \otimes b\). The heatmap appears uniform, and the transport cost may deviate from the true OT cost.
  • Small \(\varepsilon\): the coupling sharpens and concentrates mass along cost-optimal paths, recovering the unregularised plan. However, numerical underflow in \(K\) can cause instability.
  • Moderate \(\varepsilon\): balances stability with meaningful structure in the transport plan.

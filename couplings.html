Coupling Examples

Below are the six distribution pairs available in the interactive visualisation. Each pair illustrates a different aspect of discrete optimal transport. The restaurant revenue model serves as an applied illustration; the core focus of the project remains the general theory and algorithm.

Gaussian vs Gaussian

Two normal distributions parameterised by their means \(\mu_a, \mu_b\) and standard deviations \(\sigma_a, \sigma_b\). Both are discretised on a shared grid spanning \(\pm 4\) standard deviations. This example shows transport between continuous, symmetric distributions and is a good starting point for exploring the effect of \(\varepsilon\).

Gaussian vs Poisson

A Gaussian (mean \(\mu_a\), std \(\sigma_a\)) evaluated on integer support, paired with a Poisson distribution of rate \(\lambda_b\). This combination mixes a continuous density with a discrete mass function on the same integer grid, illustrating transport across different distributional types.

Poisson vs Poisson

Two Poisson distributions with rates \(\lambda_a\) and \(\lambda_b\) on a shared integer grid that covers up to \(\lambda + 6\sqrt{\lambda}\). Adjusting the rates shifts the bulk of the mass and changes the effective support size, affecting the cost matrix and the coupling.

Exponential vs Exponential

Two exponential distributions with rate parameters \(\lambda_a\) and \(\lambda_b\), both supported on \([0, \infty)\) and discretised on a shared positive grid. These distributions decay monotonically, so the coupling typically concentrates near the lower end of the support.

Random Sum Distribution

A mixture of a Gaussian and an exponential component, producing a shape that is right-skewed with a Gaussian peak. The mixing weight \(w \in [0,1]\) controls the balance between the two components. This example demonstrates transport involving a multimodal or asymmetric source distribution.

Restaurant Revenue Model

A toy applied example: daily customer arrivals (and hence revenue units) are modelled as Poisson with mean \(\lambda_a\) under a baseline pricing strategy. After a pricing or promotional change the demand shifts to \(\lambda_b\). The optimal transport plan reveals how mass is redistributed across revenue levels, providing an intuitive reading of the Wasserstein distance between the two regimes.
